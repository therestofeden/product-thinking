# How I Do Product Discovery and Write a Product Master Doc

*A walkthrough of the method I use before a single line of spec gets written — starting from mission and North Star alignment, through opportunity mapping and epistemic honesty, to kill switches and a product document that states what failure looks like before it defines success.*

---

I recently led the discovery process for a new advertising product at a major European e-commerce platform. The product — let's call it Discovery Ads — proposed extending a performance advertising format from its current home in search results into the rest of the platform: the home feed, recommendation carousels, the shopping cart, filtered catalog pages. A significant initiative. A large financial opportunity on paper. And, like most initiatives that look compelling on paper, full of assumptions that could kill it quietly if left unexamined.

This is the method I used to work through it. Not the product — the method.

---

## Step Zero: Why Do We Exist? Mission → North Star → Product Outcome

Before I open a document, before I look at data, before I frame a single opportunity, I ask three questions in sequence. They look almost philosophical. They are actually the most practical thing I do.

**Why does this product exist?** Not what it does — why it exists. What is the mission that would make the world worse off if this team stopped showing up?

**What is the North Star?** Not a KPI. The single condition that, if achieved, means the mission is being fulfilled — and that is causally connected to the company's own North Star rather than being a local metric that looks good in a team review but is orthogonal to what the business actually optimizes for.

**What does success look like for each side of the market?** In a two-sided marketplace, this question is not optional. A business that monetizes attention generated by consumers serves two distinct customers — the end users whose behavior creates the inventory, and the partners who pay to reach them. These interests are not automatically aligned. The product that maximizes short-term partner revenue by saturating every surface with ads will eventually destroy the end-user experience that makes those surfaces valuable. The product that protects end-user experience by setting floors on ad frequency may leave meaningful partner value uncaptured. The North Star has to hold both sides simultaneously, or it will optimize one at the expense of the other.

This is not abstract. It is the structural constraint that every product decision on a two-sided platform is made within — and if you don't state it explicitly before discovery begins, you will make tradeoffs implicitly, inconsistently, and invisibly.

### How I apply this in practice

For Discovery Ads, the mission of the advertising product was something like: *help brand partners grow their business on the platform by enabling them to reach the right users with the right content at the right time*. That is the partner side. But the platform's own North Star is commerce — it exists to connect shoppers with products they want to buy and to make that experience excellent. The advertising product succeeds when it serves both: when it helps partners grow, and when it does so in a way that makes the shopping experience better or at worst neutral — not worse.

That three-way relationship — platform mission, partner success, shopper experience — is what I write down before anything else. It looks like this:

| Layer | Statement |
|---|---|
| Platform North Star | Connect shoppers with products they want — make that experience excellent |
| Advertising product mission | Help brand partners drive incremental growth by reaching purchase-ready shoppers |
| Product success condition | Partner incremental ROAS is positive AND shopper conversion rate is not harmed |
| What "failure" looks like | High attributed partner revenue + degraded shopper conversion = build trap entry |

That last row is the one most product documents leave out. A retail media product can generate impressive attributed revenue while quietly destroying the shopper experience that makes the inventory worth buying. Attribution is a lagging, partial signal. Shopper conversion is the leading, honest one. If I optimize for attributed partner revenue without a guardrail on shopper conversion, I am optimizing for the map, not the territory.

Only once I have this table written do I move to the outcome sentence for the specific product I am working on. The outcome sentence is then derivable — it follows logically from the mission stack — rather than invented to fit the initiative.

For Discovery Ads: the platform's North Star is shopper-commerce quality. The advertising mission is incremental partner growth. The product outcome has to thread both: *reach purchase-bound shoppers on surfaces where we previously had no presence, and demonstrate that this reach creates genuine incremental value for partners — without degrading the conversion quality that makes those surfaces worth monetizing at all.*

Every decision that followed — which surfaces to prioritize, which metrics to treat as guardrails vs. primary signals, where to put kill switches — derived from that stack. Not from the financial model. Not from what the team could build. From why we exist and what we are trying to do for both sides of the market.

This step takes a few hours. It saves weeks of misaligned speccing.

## Start with the Territory, Not the Map

The first trap in product work is treating the problem definition as settled. A VP says "we should expand our ads beyond search." An analyst produces a deck showing unmonetized surfaces. A financial model appears, projecting tens of millions in incremental revenue. Everyone nods. Engineering asks when to start.

This is a map. It may or may not correspond to the territory.

Before I write anything that looks like a spec, I spend time on one question: *what do we actually know, versus what do we believe, versus what are we assuming?*

I borrow a labeling convention from the scientific literature and apply it aggressively to every factual claim in the discovery document:

- **[KNOWN]** — empirically validated. An A/B result with a clean holdout, a peer-reviewed finding, a number from our own database that has been QA'd.
- **[BELIEVED]** — theory-grounded and proxy-evidenced. We have a mechanism, we have analogous evidence, but we haven't validated it directly at this system.
- **[ASSUMED]** — implicit and unvalidated. Required for the logic to hold, but not yet interrogated.

On Discovery Ads, when I ran this exercise on the financial model, here is what I found:

The coverage gap — the fact that roughly 60% of purchase-bound sessions occur on surfaces we don't currently monetize — was **[KNOWN]**. We had the platform session data. We had traced user paths from session entry to purchase across three months of GA data, event by event. The number was robust.

The conservative financial projection — €25M in incremental ad revenue over three months — was **[BELIEVED]**. We applied a 50% CVR penalty to account for the fact that loose-intent surfaces convert differently than search. But the 50% was a judgment call, not a measurement. We had never run ads on the home feed or the cart. We had no prior data on what CVR would look like there. We called it conservative, but "conservative" in the absence of any baseline is just a more palatable kind of assumption.

The pilot partner quote — "35% increase in incremental revenue at stable ROAS" — was **[ASSUMED]**. A single quote from a single managed-service partner. Not independently validated, not from a randomized holdout, not reproducible as evidence.

Most product documents I encounter don't distinguish between these three. The financial model is presented as a forecast. The pilot quote is cited as validation. The assumed CVR is labeled conservative. And then when the experiment runs and the numbers don't match, the team is surprised — when they shouldn't be, because they never knew what kind of claim they were making.

The labeling forces intellectual honesty before commitment. It also tells you exactly where your discovery work needs to go next: toward the [ASSUMED] claims, which are the ones that will hurt you most if they turn out to be wrong.

## Define the Outcome Before You Name the Problem

Before I identify a single opportunity, I write one sentence. One sentence that defines what the product is supposed to accomplish — for a specific customer, in a way that is measurable, in a way that is framed as an outcome and not an output.

This is the hardest sentence in the document to write. And it is the most important.

For Discovery Ads, I spent considerable time on this. The instinct was to write something like: "Discovery Ads extends SPA to new surfaces." That is an output. It describes what we ship, not what changes in the world as a result. Marty Cagan calls this the output trap — and Melissa Perri gives it a name too: the build trap. Optimizing for what you ship rather than what you change.

The outcome I arrived at was:

> *Discovery Ads enables brand partners to reach purchase-bound shoppers across the full shopping journey — not just in search contexts — such that partners can drive incremental conversions on surfaces where performance advertising did not previously exist, while maintaining the ROAS efficiency that makes reinvestment rational.*

Every word is load-bearing. "Purchase-bound" — not all shoppers, not all impressions. "Incremental conversions" — not attributed GMV, not clicks. "Surfaces where performance advertising did not previously exist" — the counterfactual is explicit. "ROAS efficiency that makes reinvestment rational" — the advertiser's flywheel is in the definition.

This sentence then does something powerful: it tells you what you're not trying to do. You're not trying to maximize impressions. You're not trying to maximize attributed revenue. You're not trying to run ads on every possible surface regardless of what happens to conversion rates or partner trust. The outcome definition is a scope filter.

And when I get to measurement — which comes immediately after the outcome statement, not later — I organize metrics in a hierarchy that flows directly from that sentence:

**Primary (causal)**: Incremental ROAS, measured via holdout. The only metric that directly proves the outcome sentence is true.

**Secondary (within experiment window)**: Attributed GMV by surface. Acceptable as a rollout gate provided we have documented the assumed relationship between attributed and incremental at this scale.

**Guardrail (co-primary for the highest-risk phase)**: Platform checkout conversion rate. Because introducing ads into the shopping cart might harm the thing the product is meant to serve — and if it does, no amount of ad revenue compensates.

**Leading indicators (2–4 week signal)**: CTR on new placements, add-to-cart rate from sponsored units. Because a quarter without a leading indicator is a quarter without a feedback loop.

Notice the guardrail metric for cart is co-primary, not secondary. This is deliberate. When I got to the cart phase of Discovery Ads, 79% of the total financial opportunity lived there. If you're writing a document where 79% of the upside depends on one phase not breaking the checkout funnel, the metric that measures whether you're breaking the checkout funnel is not a guardrail — it is a primary measure of success.

Defining metrics this way forces the team to decide, before the experiment launches, what a "good result" looks like. And it forces the team to decide what a "bad result" looks like. Which brings me to the part of discovery I find most undervalued.

## Define Failure Before You Define Success — and Give It a Number

Most product documents define success. Very few define failure. And the ones that do define failure rarely give it a specific number.

I call these kill switches, and I require one for every phase of a phased rollout. Not "we'll monitor and adjust." A pre-agreed threshold, documented before the experiment launches, that triggers a specific action if crossed.

On Discovery Ads, the kill switch structure looked like this:

**Phase 1 (Home Feed + Recommendation Carousels)**: If CTR or CVR on new placements is more than 50% below the search SPA baseline, we pause the rollout, diagnose URM quality, and do not advance to Phase 3 regardless of timeline pressure. Phase 1 is the URM validation sandbox — if it fails there, the underlying prediction model is not ready for the cart.

**Phase 2 (Catalog Filter Bypass)**: If any device (Web, iOS, Android) shows a measurable signal of filter confusion — users resetting filters immediately after ad exposure, exit rates elevating post-ad-impression — Phase 2 halts. The design must be re-validated before it proceeds. No exceptions. This was the stated number-one failure mode for the entire initiative: if the new visual design fails to communicate clearly that the user's filter is intact and the ad is a separate sponsored layer, we erode platform trust. Platform trust is harder to rebuild than ad revenue.

**Phase 3 (Cart)**: A pre-agreed checkout abandonment threshold — the specific number to be defined with Finance and Platform before Phase 3 launches — triggers immediate disablement. This threshold was, as of the discovery document I wrote, an open blocker. It did not yet exist. I named it as such explicitly in the document, in the open risks register.

That last point matters. In a discovery document, an open blocker is not a gap you minimize or phrase softly. It is something you state plainly, name the owner for, and attach a deadline to. The kill switch for Phase 3 needed a number. That number did not exist. The document said so in unambiguous terms. Not because it was embarrassing, but because unnamed blockers are the things that slip through and become production incidents.

The discipline here comes from what Cagan calls the pre-mortem — asking "what are the three most likely ways this fails?" before you build anything. In my discovery documents I turn that into a formal Open Risks Register: every significant risk with a severity rating, an owner, a status, and a next action. Not a retrospective inventory of what went wrong. A prospective one of what could.

## Map the Opportunity Space Before You Name Any Solution

Once I have the outcome statement and the epistemic inventory, I build what Melissa Torres calls an Opportunity Solution Tree. I use it because it enforces a discipline that is very easy to violate: opportunities must be validated before solutions are considered, and solutions must be linked explicitly to specific validated opportunities.

The OST starts with the desired outcome at the root — the sentence I wrote above. From that root, I identify opportunities. And I am strict about what counts as an opportunity.

An opportunity is a value gap: a specific place where the product fails to deliver its stated outcome for a specific customer segment, with a hypothesis about why. It is not a feature. It is not a solution. It is not a capability the team wants to build.

"Add cart placement" is not an opportunity. It is a solution. The opportunity it might address is: "Purchase-bound shoppers arriving at checkout have been building purchase intent across their session, but no performance advertising mechanism exists that can reach them at that moment — 53% of all pre-purchase events on the platform happen on a surface we cannot touch." That is an opportunity. It names the customer state, the gap, the mechanism of the gap, and the downstream consequence.

This distinction matters enormously for prioritization. When you frame opportunities as value gaps rather than features, you can compare them honestly. The home feed opportunity addresses 14 million user sessions at the entry point of the shopping journey. The cart opportunity addresses 53% of pre-purchase events but introduces checkout abandonment risk that could permanently disable the surface. The catalog filter bypass opportunity addresses cross-sell in high-intent sessions but depends on an unproven assumption about design legibility across device types. These have materially different risk profiles, evidence levels, and dependency structures — and you can only see that clearly if you've framed them as opportunities, not as a feature list.

For each opportunity in the tree, I require the same four things:

1. **The gap**: what specifically fails, for whom, through what mechanism, with what downstream consequence.
2. **The evidence**: labeled [KNOWN], [BELIEVED], or [ASSUMED].
3. **The Four Risks** (Cagan): Value risk, Usability risk, Feasibility risk, Viability risk — each rated H/M/L with a one-line evidence statement. Any High rating without a named mitigation path is a blocker to speccing.
4. **The riskiest assumption**: the single assumption that, if wrong, invalidates the solution-opportunity link entirely. Not the full assumption register — the one that kills the proposal if it fails.

The riskiest assumption test is where a lot of feature work falls apart. For the cart placement, the riskiest assumption was not "will advertisers pay for it?" or "can we build it?" The riskiest assumption was: "adding a sponsored product carousel to the shopping cart does not meaningfully increase checkout abandonment." That assumption, if wrong, does not reduce ad revenue — it damages core commerce. Getting that wrong at scale is irreversible in a way that most product failures are not. Naming it clearly, before building, is the only way to make sure the experiment is designed to test it.

## Make the Financial Model a Belief System, Not a Forecast

Product financial models are almost always presented as forecasts. They have specific numbers. They have best-case and conservative scenarios. They look precise.

They are belief systems. And the discipline is to treat them as such.

For Discovery Ads, the financial model projected €25–50M in incremental ad revenue per quarter depending on scenario. Those numbers came from a volume uplift calculation grounded in real platform path data — that part was [KNOWN]. But the conversion rate assumptions that turned event volume into revenue were [BELIEVED] at best, [ASSUMED] at worst.

In the conservative scenario, we applied a 50% CVR penalty to all new surfaces. Why 50%? Because it felt like a significant enough haircut to be defensible. Not because we had measured CVR on the home feed or the cart. We had never run ads there.

The honest framing of the financial model is: "here are the conditions under which this initiative is worth pursuing, here is what we would need to be true about CVR on new surfaces, and here is what the experiment needs to tell us before we make any financial commitment at scale." The model's job is not to predict the outcome — it is to make the assumptions explicit enough that you know which ones the experiment needs to invalidate or confirm.

One structural observation from the Discovery Ads model that I think is worth naming: 79% of the projected financial upside lived in Phase 3 (Cart). The home feed and catalog bypass together accounted for roughly 21%. This means the initiative was not three phases with roughly equal weight — it was a discovery process for two phases that existed primarily to de-risk and enable a third phase on which almost the entire financial case depended.

Once you see that, the prioritization logic changes. Phase 1 is not "first because it's lower risk and a good starting point." Phase 1 is the URM validation sandbox that determines whether the prediction model is mature enough to operate context-free — because if it isn't, Phase 3 should not launch, and Phase 3 is where all the money is. Phase 1 is risk mitigation for Phase 3, and the document should say so explicitly.

## The Document That Results: What a Product Master Doc Actually Is

A Product Master Doc is not a PRD. It is not a spec. It contains no acceptance criteria. It commits to no implementation details. It does not tell engineering what to build.

What it does:

**It states the outcome before naming any solution.** Every reader — engineer, scientist, exec, partner-facing PM — starts from the same sentence about what the product is supposed to accomplish.

**It makes the epistemic inventory visible.** [KNOWN], [BELIEVED], [ASSUMED] labels on every major claim. This is the alignment layer between the map and the territory. If the team is going to disagree, they should disagree about epistemics — about what kind of claim we're making — not about facts.

**It maps the opportunity space from the outcome down.** The OST structure means every opportunity is traceable to the outcome statement. Every solution is traceable to a specific validated opportunity. If you can't draw that line, you haven't done the discovery.

**It defines failure before success.** Kill switches for every phase. A metric hierarchy that includes guardrails, not just primary metrics. Open blockers named with owners and deadlines.

**It documents what the team doesn't know.** An Open Risks Register with severity, owner, status, and next action. The highest-severity risks are called out explicitly — not buried in footnotes, not softened in language.

**It is a living document until the pilot reads out.** Every phase gate updates it. Every open risk that closes or escalates updates it. It is not a snapshot — it is the team's continuously maintained picture of what we know and what we're betting on.

## Why This Matters

The real function of this process is to protect the team from two failure modes that are far more common than building the wrong feature:

The first is **false precision** — presenting [ASSUMED] claims as [KNOWN] ones, financial projections as forecasts, convenient numbers as evidence. False precision produces confident roadmaps for products that fail, and it produces that confidence in a way that makes the failure hard to diagnose after the fact because no one ever wrote down what was actually assumed.

The second is **premature commitment** — starting to spec and build before the riskiest assumptions have been tested. The build trap, as Perri describes it, is not entered when you build the wrong thing. It is entered at the moment you commit to building before you've validated the opportunity. A team that has spent six months building a cart advertising placement without a pre-agreed kill switch for checkout abandonment, and without a holdout designed to distinguish incremental from attributed GMV, is in the build trap even if the attributed numbers look good.

The Product Master Doc is the mechanism for delaying that commitment until it's earned. Not indefinitely — but until the discovery work has done its job: validating the opportunity, surfacing the riskiest assumptions, designing the experiments that will test them, and defining what failure looks like before it happens.

## The Frameworks, Named

The method above draws on three books that I think every product manager should own:

**Marty Cagan — *Inspired*.** The Four Risks framework (Value, Usability, Feasibility, Viability) as a gate before speccing. The missionary vs. mercenary distinction — the idea that a product team with a clear mission makes categorically better decisions than a team executing a stakeholder wish list. The insistence that discovery and delivery run in parallel, not sequentially.

**Melissa Torres — *Continuous Discovery Habits*.** The Opportunity Solution Tree as the default structure for mapping a problem space. The discipline of opportunity-first framing — the insistence that solutions are hypotheses, not commitments, until the opportunity is validated.

**Melissa Perri — *Escaping the Build Trap*.** The outcome-over-output framing. The Product Kata. The strategy deployment chain — Vision → Strategic Intent → Product Initiative — which is the formal version of what the Mission → North Star → Product Outcome stack in the first section is doing. The recognition that the build trap is entered not at the moment of building but at the moment of premature commitment — and that the antidote is outcome clarity before solution commitment.

None of these frameworks work in isolation. The OST without the Four Risks produces a well-structured opportunity map that never asks whether the solutions are buildable or viable. The Four Risks without epistemic labeling produces a risk assessment that feels rigorous but treats all claims as equivalent. The outcome framing without kill switches produces a product with a clear north star and no mechanism for knowing when to stop.

The method above is an attempt to make them work together — applied to a real initiative, in the real constraints of a commercial product team, where the pressure to build is constant and the time for discovery is never quite enough.
